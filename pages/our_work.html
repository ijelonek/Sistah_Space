Many UK born or raised African heritage people grew up at at time when only white men were , teachers, headteachers, vicars, bank managers, police. It was almost unheard of for an African heritage woman or man to be a marriage registrar or hold any other similar role. Any high profile jobs prodominalty done by white males. In contrast, African heritage people were doing all the menial jobs, like cleaning, factory work and in particular, railway and London Transport jobs. The well known phrase, no blacks, no Irish, no dogs was in many windows in 1950's and 60's Britain. Many of the children of that time are now the adults of this time and although much has changed in terms of work opportunites, equality for all has not been achieved. In addition, there was not much support for any person suffering domestic abuse, it was very much considered a 'domestic' affair to be sorted out by the persons involved. This did not bode well for women in particular. African heritage women even less so. The repercussions of that era are still very much felt today, with many African heritage victims of abuse, extremely relunctant to report crime to the police or other agencies. The reasons given are many and include, Discrimination Not being taken seriously by police Not trusting Social Service will not blame them and take away their children Not wanting the African heritage man to be beaten or killed by police (a very real fear to this day. Although he might be considered abusive, the survivor still had concerns for their safety) Unresolved issues around displacement and slavery Services not understanding African and Caribbean culture, and therefore not suitable for African heritage survivors. Insufficient adequate resources or places that reflect their cultural heritage.
